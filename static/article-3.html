TRAILS IN RUBY RAILS- CONVENTION OVER CONFIGURATION!

BY: Daniel Brito

Visualize yourself in a maze. Blindfolded. You may have been here before, and you can kinda guess that you have to get to the center. The path to your goal is disorienting and treacherous. You could try to break through the walls enough times to do this but that might leave you hurt and it might just be too much work. No. You know what to do here. It’s left, a right, left, left right left and in a jiffy you are where you need to be. Many have taken this path, those that have been there have bestowed upon you the knowledge. No struggle needed.

Snapping back to reality, unfortunately, we are not wizards traversing the mystical unknown, warding off evils, fighting our way through a maze until we find the golden egg… darn! Probably just a coding dream, a metaphor for passing your rspecs, following convention over configuration in the attempt to access the golden utopia of having your code work. We all do things differently, we are all individuals. But many of us follow a certain pattern because they have shown repeated results. We drive with one foot, so we can avoid confusing the brake and gas pedals. We paint up and down to finish quickly, instead of left and right. And we take the same way to work/school every day because, for the most part, it’s what gets us there the quickest.

Convention

Enough analogies. As the godfather of rails imagined and ultimately created for our use, rails heavily implements most of its code for certain conventions that iron out any complications and long drawn out coding, making our lives easier. Following these conventions, primarily naming and file structures, effectively allows us to have files layed out for that would otherwise have taken time to create and type out. As soon as you type out rails new — api you have your rails back-end.

Not only is this done for us. but defining routes in the way templated by rails is a one-liner.

resources model_name
takes care of your crud routes, leaving you only to define its methods within your controllers. Bear in mind that doing this requires you to use the method naming conventions e.g for your GET actions you need to have methods #index (for retrieving all entries for a table) and #show (for retrieving a specific entry). Imagine having your code mostly typed out for you! imagine no more.

Configuration

Bear also in mind that we are developers. So we do much tweaking and tailoring to our needs. There is many a case in which we would need to create custom methods that using the conventions simply do not provide. But we as developers also love reusability, and that's something that (if following RESTful conventions) allows for. Aside from that, deviating from the conventions leaves a lot of room for guessing and testing your code, which given any time constraints is not what you want. Moreover working with a team begs for a general consensus on how this code should look, what better way than one that we probably have all probably learned. Your custom methods are better spent on handling those special cases should they arise.

Conclusion

With this awareness of the time you could be saving, you are better equipped for any project in that you’ll be able to easily work with others, and very quickly put together things without having to memorize so much. Giving you time after to focus on handling special cases. Not just on rails, but in any language, library, or framework, knowing what the conventions are, gives you the predictability your code needs.
